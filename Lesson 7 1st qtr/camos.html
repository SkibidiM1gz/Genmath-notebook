TRANSFORMATION OF FUNCTIONS

Often when given a problem, we try to model the scenario using mathematics in the form
of words, tables, graphs and equations in order to explain or solve it. When building
models, it is often helpful to build off of existing formulas or models. Knowing the basic
graphs of your functions can help you solve problems by being able to model new
behavior by adapting something you already know. Unfortunately, the models and
existing formulas we know are not always exactly the same as the ones presented in the
problems we face.

Fortunately, there are systematic ways to shift, stretch, compress, flip and combine
functions to help them become better models for the problems we are trying to solve. We
can transform what we already know into what we need, hence the name,
‚ÄúTransformation of functions.‚Äù When we have a story problem, formula, graph, or table,
we can then transform that function in a variety of ways to form new functions.

TRANSFORMATION OF FUNCTIONS: EXAMPLE 1

What transformations of the original function f(x) are involved in the following? Write the transformations in the correct order.

a(x) = -f(x + 2)

Answer: Reflect about x-axis, shift 2 units to the left

b(x) = 2f(-x) + 9

Answer: Reflect about y-axis, vertical stretch by a scale factor of 2, shift 9 units upwards

c(x) = - (1/5) f(x - 2) - 1

Answer: Reflect about x-axis, vertical compress by a scale factor of 1/5, shift 2 units to the right, shift 1 unit downwards

TRANSFORMATION OF FUNCTIONS: EXAMPLE 1 (cont.)

What transformations of the original function f(x) are involved in the following? Write the transformations in the correct order.

d(x) = f(3 - x)

Answer:

First, rewrite the function.

d(x) = f(3 - x) = f(-x + 3) = f(-(x - 3))

Notice that we factored out the negative sign for us to know what really happened to x.

Our final answer should be:

REFLECT ABOUT Y-AXIS ‚Üí because x is negative
